Automating Lasercom Terminal Camera Calibration

Data transmission using lasers is complicated. High frame-rate infrared cameras must precisely capture light beams sent under any atmospheric condition, in the span of hundreds of miles. This is accomplished by precisely calibrating lasercom terminals via a series of manual hardware configuration tests. During Summer 2019, myself and a team of 3 other interns at MIT Lincoln Laboratory built a system to automate the calibration process, reducing the time it takes to run a test series from 2-3 weeks, to overnight completion.

Code for Good

In October 2019, I was introduced with 4 other CS undergrads at J.P. Morgan Chase & Co's 'Code for Good' Hackathon, before being pit against 13 other teams to build a program that fulfilled the needs of a non-profit organization, in 24 hours. Taking charge of our team, I broke our build period down into sprints as we began work in bolstering user engagement and increasing the likelihood of our client's users in graduating from their unique debt management programs. After rotations by tech leads and our clients, our team was one of four selected for the final presentation stage. After 30 hours awake and final presentations, we were awarded second place for our client.

Improving American Sign Language Recognition with Facial Features

Current approaches to the machine translation of American Sign Language neglect essential language nuances, data-processing techniques, and make decisions based on the preoccupations of the hearing community, instead of being designed in a user-centric manner. In Summer 2019, I led a team of 6 interns at MIT Lincoln Laboratory to win the I3C Challenge, recieving funding to continue our project and work for the Lab during the academic year, researching and developing optimal approaches towards full ASL translation.
