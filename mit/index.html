Overview

Current approaches to the machine translation of American Sign Language (ASL) neglect essential language nuances, data-processing techniques, and make decisions based on the preoccupations of the hearing community.

Background

Towards the end of my internship at Lincoln, I led a team to win the annual MIT I3C Competition, from an initial 32 teams. After each workday, I would return home and work on a comprehensive system to improve the machine translation of ASL with a design informed by Signing communities. I recruited fellow interns to my team, and after success in several pitch rounds and technical poster presentations, we received ‘Best Poster’ from the Lab staff and secured funding for development during the academic year.

ENVOY: Enabling Natural-language Versatility & Opportunity

Description

Over the last two years, I have focused my team’s efforts to better-distinguish ASL signs that vary slightly in presentation but hold different meanings. A computationally-expensive process, we train models and leverage the capabilities of the Satori supercomputing cluster. ASL translation is an intellectually demanding endeavor, as several individually-difficult areas must work in harmony. We implement computer vision to run our system, capturing users’ movements and facial features in real-time. This year, I worked on feature extraction and natural language processing for language modeling, as the conversion of ASL to English is not a one-to-one translation.

We faced struggles early on due to the limitations of pre-existing databases containing studio-quality translation videos, which neglected variations in lighting, background, and signer skin tone. To ensure an equitable design, I am employing novel data mining and augmentation techniques; finding automated methods to generate labelled datasets from CSPAN footage and other public sources, while expanding the diversity of signers. I further fortify our datasets by training our convolutional network models against fake data generated from adversarial networks.
